Bitchat ‚Äì A decentralized messaging app that works over Bluetooth mesh networks#

Title: Introducing Bitchat: A Decentralized, Secure Messaging App for Developers

In the ever-evolving landscape of messaging apps, a new contender has emerged: Bitchat. This open-source, decentralized application uses Bluetooth mesh networks to facilitate secure, peer-to-peer communication without relying on servers or internet connection.

Released under public domain, Bitchat offers numerous features that matter for software developers and AI engineers seeking privacy and security in their communication tools. These include end-to-end encryption, room-based chats with optional password protection, store & forward functionality, and IRC-style commands for familiarity.

Key technologies employed by Bitchat include X25519 key exchange + AES-256-GCM for private messages, Argon2id password derivation + AES-256-GCM for room messages, and Ed25519 digital signatures to ensure message authenticity. Forward secrecy is also implemented, ensuring new key pairs are generated each session.

Bitchat offers universal app support for iOS and macOS, and three setup options: using XcodeGen (recommended), Swift Package Manager, or manual Xcode Project creation.

In terms of privacy, Bitchat does not require registration, employs ephemeral messages by default, covers traffic with random delays and dummy messages, and includes an emergency wipe feature to instantly clear all data. It operates locally without servers involved, ensuring complete offline functionality.

Performance optimizations include LZ4 message compression for bandwidth savings, adaptive power modes based on battery level, automatic power saving when backgrounded, configurable scanning, optimized Bloom filters for faster duplicate detection, and message aggregation to reduce transmissions.

Developers interested in getting started with Bitchat can follow the provided setup instructions, and familiarize themselves with the app’s basic commands, room features, and security & privacy mechanisms. The protocol design is platform-agnostic, making an Android client a viable future extension of this innovative project.

Researchers Attempt to Uncover the Origins of Creativity in Diffusion Models#

In a groundbreaking development, Stanford researchers Mason Kamb and Surya Ganguli have proposed a mechanism that explains the creativity of diffusion models, a key advancement for software developers and AI engineers working with generative models. The research suggests that creativity in these models is a deterministic consequence of their denoising process, which involves learning a score function to uncover images from noise.

The researchers identified two inductive biases, translational equivariance, and locality, in the models’ use of convolutional neural networks (CNNs). Translational equivariance refers to the model’s tendency to reflect shifts in the input image, while locality arises from the CNNs considering only a small neighborhood of input pixels.

Building on these insights, they developed an Equivariant Local Score (ELS) machine, a mathematical model that optimizes a score function for equivariance and locality. The ELS machine showed remarkable accuracy in comparing its outputs with those of diffusion models such as ResNets and UNets trained on simplified models, achieving an accuracy of around 90%.

The study explains how diffusion models create new images by mixing and matching local training set image patches, offering insights into their creative process and potential errors. The researchers’ findings are significant because they provide the first analytical theory to explain a deep neural network-based generative model’s creative outputs with such accuracy.

The researchers also shared the code they used in their study, opening up opportunities for further exploration of this intriguing area of AI and diffusion models. This research could form the foundation for studying more complex diffusion models, particularly those that incorporate self-attention layers.

Meta‚Äôs Full-stack HHVM optimizations for GenAI#

Title: Optimizing Infrastructure for Generative AI at Meta: A Web Foundation Perspective

In a recent development, Meta has launched innovative products utilizing Generative AI (GenAI), necessitating an evolution of its underlying infrastructure components to keep pace. The article discusses how Meta’s Web Foundation team optimized their monolithic web tier, running on Hack, to accommodate GenAI requirements without compromising system sustainability or user experience.

The team employed a series of best practices and optimizations, including:

  1. Isolating GenAI inference traffic into a dedicated WWW tenant for specialized runtime and warm-up configuration.
  2. Increasing the request timeout limit on GenAI requests to avoid timing out and adverse effects on other products.
  3. Allocating more worker threads (approximately 1000) on GenAI hosts compared to normal webservers due to longer request durations.
  4. Utilizing Just-In-Time (JIT) cache and Jump-Start techniques for faster execution, even with reduced code overlap.
  5. Implementing request warm-up to prefetch configuration values, service discovery info, and non-code caches within the webserver, reducing initial fetch latencies.
  6. Adding request shadowing to ensure gating changes are still covered in the JIT cache during steady state.

These optimizations have resulted in a 30% improvement in GenAI latency, demonstrating the importance of infrastructure adaptability for software developers and AI engineers working with evolving technologies like GenAI. The insights gained from this experience can guide other organizations navigating similar challenges in integrating generative AI into their product offerings.
